In recent years, organizations have increasingly adopted data‑driven decision‑making,
assuming that more data automatically leads to better outcomes. However,
several studies reveal a paradox: teams with access to excessive data often
make slower and less accurate decisions. This phenomenon is partly due to
“analysis paralysis,” where decision‑makers spend disproportionate time
reconciling conflicting data points instead of identifying the core variables
that truly influence outcomes.



Moreover, the reliability of data itself is frequently
overestimated. Many companies collect data from systems that were never
designed for analytical precision. For example, operational logs may capture
events inconsistently, and customer feedback systems may attract responses only
from highly satisfied or highly dissatisfied users, creating skewed datasets.
When such data is fed into predictive models, the resulting insights may appear
statistically sound but lack real‑world validity.



Another challenge arises from the assumption that
correlation implies causation. In fast‑changing environments, variables that
once moved together may diverge due to shifts in consumer behavior, regulatory
changes, or technological disruptions. Yet decision‑makers, under pressure to
justify their choices, often rely on outdated correlations because they appear
mathematically convincing.



To address these issues, some organizations have begun
emphasizing “data minimalism,” a practice that prioritizes a small set of high‑quality
indicators over large volumes of loosely relevant data. Early adopters report
that teams become more decisive, models become more interpretable, and
strategic discussions shift from debating numbers to evaluating assumptions.
Interestingly, these organizations also observe an improvement in innovation,
as employees feel less constrained by rigid dashboards and more empowered to
explore unconventional ideas.



Ultimately, the passage suggests that data is valuable not
by its quantity but by its clarity, context, and alignment with the decisions
it aims to support.
